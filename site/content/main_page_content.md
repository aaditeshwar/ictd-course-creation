# ICTD: Information and Communication Technologies for Development and Sustainability

**July-December, 2026**

**Instructor: Aaditeshwar Seth, IIT Delhi**

## Why this course, now

There is a reasonable argument to be made that this is an unusually good time to be learning how to build technology for the hardest problems in the world. But that argument only holds up if we're honest about what computing will be able to solve, and where it will need to stretch itself. This course is built at the intersection of three claims.

**First: problem discovery is not something AI is about to automate away.** In ["AI as Normal Technology"](https://knightcolumbia.org/content/ai-as-normal-technology) and a subsequent talk ["What Will Be Left for Us to Work On",](https://www.cs.princeton.edu/~arvindn/talks/icml-2026-annotated-slides/) Arvind Narayanan and Sayash Kapoor argue that the bottleneck on AI's real-world impact is overwhelmingly on the *application* side: knowing which problem is worth solving, for whom, and under what constraints. 

**Second: knowing what to build is not enough -- you have to build it to bring power-based equality, not just efficiency.** In my book, *[Technology and (Dis)Empowerment: A Call to Technologists](https://www.cse.iitd.ernet.in/~aseth/act.html)*, I makes the case that "social good" cannot just mean privacy guardrails or "do no harm" -- it has to be a real, consequentialist commitment to redistributing power, and that achieving it requires technologists to stay connected with their users. The course borrows its ethical spine directly from the book: every project you build here should be able to answer *whose power does this shift, and towards what*.

**Third: none of this is a reason to under-invest in depth.** It's tempting to read the first two points and conclude that AI handles the "how" so you can coast on the "why." The frontier of deployed technology is jagged, not smooth, and the only way to know which side of it you're standing on is to actually understand the domain and the technique well enough to catch the model when it's confidently wrong. Erik Brynjolfsson makes a related point in ["The Turing Trap"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.04200): technology should be built to *augment* human judgment and depth is what lets you be augmented rather than replaced -- by the model, and by whoever owns it.

Taken together: this course assumes AI is a genuinely useful tool you should use throughout the course, and it is squarely focused on the three things that remain the hardest and most valuable parts of the pipeline regardless of how good the tools get -- understanding a problem in its real context, building toward power-equality rather than just efficiency, and building grounded and robust systems which you understand at depth.

## How the course is organized: areas and topics

Rural and low-income communities in developing regions face problems that recur across sectors but demand genuinely different technical and human approaches depending on the sector. We organize the course as a matrix of **areas** (the sectors and domains where technology gets applied) and **topics** (the research and engineering skills that generalize across any area you apply them to). A handful of **cross-cutting axes** -- gender, caste, social capital and marginalization, and climate change -- run through every area and every topic because who benefits and who is left out is never a side question.

### The topics: skills you will build regardless of which area you work in

1. **Problem Discovery** — Deeply understanding the context, constraints, incentives, and existing systems of the people you're building for, before you assume you know what problem you're solving. This includes theoretical grounding (the capability approach, appropriate technology,  critiques of growth-centered development) as much as fieldwork.
2. **Solving with Computing Fundamentals** — Applying real computer science -- data science, machine learning (including remote sensing and computer vision), systems, and networking -- to the problem you've discovered, built to withstand the actual constraints of the context (patchy connectivity, low-end devices, literacy, language).
3. **Ethnographic Design & Iteration** — Translating a technical approach into something people will actually use, through participatory and user-centered design, and iterating through real field testing and pilots rather than a single design pass.
4. **Socio-technical Dynamics at Scale** — Understanding what happens once something is deployed for real: how errors get handled, how power and inequality get exacerbated or countered, how intermediaries and bureaucracies reshape the system in practice, and whether the intended good outcomes are actually showing up.
5. **Impact Evaluation** — Formally evaluating whether an intervention worked, using statistical and causal-inference methods (RCTs, difference-in-differences, and others) rather than assuming good intentions imply good outcomes.
6. **Operations & Scaling** — Understanding the institutional and organizational machinery needed to take something from a validated pilot to something that survives at scale: staffing, partnerships, financing, and long-term maintenance.

## What you'll do

You will work in groups of up to two. Each group will pick one of the example domains on the Examples page as a starting point, or propose your own area/domain grounded in your own understanding of a real problem. From there, your project needs two things in equal measure: strong computing fundamentals -- the technical work has to actually be good -- and real domain knowledge of the area you're working in, built through the problem-discovery skills this course teaches. A technically brilliant system built on a wrong understanding of the problem is not a good project here, and neither is a well-researched problem statement with no technical substance behind it. You can choose domains that will make use of your favourite computing subjects, or subjects you are keen to learn; and similarly you can choose areas that you feel you are most passionate for.
